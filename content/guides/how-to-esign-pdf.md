The print-sign-scan loop is one of the most pointless steps left in modern paperwork. For most everyday documents — NDAs, lease addenda, freelance contracts, school forms — a typed or drawn electronic signature is enough.

## 1. Know what kind of signature you actually need

- **Simple e-signature** (typed name, drawn signature, or uploaded image) — fine for most business agreements and contracts between parties who trust each other.
- **Advanced/qualified electronic signature** (with identity verification and a certificate) — needed for some government filings and contracts in regulated industries.

Most day-to-day signing falls into the first category.

## 2. Add your signature to the PDF

1. Open [ClarixPDF's e-sign tool](https://clarixpdf.com/sign).
2. Upload the document.
3. Create your signature — type it, draw it with your mouse/trackpad, or upload a photo of your handwritten signature on white paper.
4. Drag the signature onto the correct line or field. Add the date if the document needs one.
5. Download the signed PDF.

## 3. Save your signature for next time

Most tools let you save a signature so you don't have to recreate it every time. Worth doing if you sign documents regularly — it turns a multi-step process into a couple of clicks.

## 4. Sending a document for someone else to sign

1. Add your own signature first if you're signing as well.
2. Send the file via email or a shared link, with clear instructions on where to sign.
3. Ask them to confirm once signed, and keep the final signed copy in a clearly labeled folder.

## A note on validity

Electronic signature laws vary by country and document type. For routine business documents, typed and drawn signatures are widely recognized under ESIGN in the US, eIDAS in the EU, and similar frameworks elsewhere. For high-stakes documents, confirm the required signature standard with the relevant authority before relying on a basic e-signature.
